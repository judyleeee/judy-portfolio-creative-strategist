Samsung.com:
From 40 seconds to 15.

How verification-first information design cut task completion time by 62% and lifted CSAT by 33% for millions of Samsung.com users.

Problem

Samsung.com's Manuals & Downloads page served millions of users globally · a post-purchase touchpoint where brand loyalty either holds or fractures. At scale, it was fracturing.

Most users were abandoning self-service and escalating to support. Every failed lookup converted into a support contact · a downstream cost compounding into support overhead, churn risk, and eroded brand trust at the exact moment that determines long-term loyalty.

Research

A dedicated UX Research team surfaced the structural mismatch driving abandonment. I partnered with them to translate the findings into design decisions.

The system required a product code (e.g. SM-F721BLBGEUB) to surface any manual. Users arrived knowing a product name at best · often only a category name. The gap between user vocabulary and system architecture was the real failure.

The gap broke along product lines · two user populations failing for structurally different reasons.

Population A · Mobile Arrives knowing a product name "Flip4" · "A53"

Could search directly · but near-identical variants broke confidence at the last step.

Population B · TV & Appliances Arrives with a category only "Smart TV" · "fridge"

Needed guided selection · no path existed from category to model.

Four recurring failure modes mapped to this gap:

40+ seconds and multiple clicks to reach a single manual
Unclear differentiation between near-identical variants
Confusing category taxonomy across product lines
Thumbnails that failed to confirm the right model

"Each one looks almost the same · I can't tell which is mine."

Hypothesis

The surface story was "navigation is broken." The deeper pattern was different · every pain point traced back to one moment: users couldn't confirm they were looking at their product.

Users didn't need more options or smarter search · they needed confirmation at the decision moment. Reduce cognitive load there · not before it · and completion should rise.

Alternatives considered and ruled out: a pure search-only fix (ignores users who don't know the model name) · a deeper taxonomy (adds clicks, worsens the 40s metric). The path forward had to serve both audiences in parallel.

Approach

One redesign · three named UI patterns working in parallel. Each pattern targeted a specific failure mode uncovered in research.

Pattern 01 · Fast path Simplified Search & Predictive Hints

Users type a product name · autocomplete surfaces the right model with imagery · cutting trial-and-error for users who know what they own.

Pattern 02 · Guided path Hierarchical Category Selection

Category taxonomy rebuilt so every step narrows confidently · scalable across Samsung's full global catalog without custom work per product line.

Pattern 03 · Verification Quick-Verify UI

Product imagery shown at the selection moment · confirming "this is mine" before the user commits · resolving near-identical variants at point of decision.

Constraint Samsung Design Guidelines compliance

Reused existing components wherever possible · kept implementation consistent and minimized dev overhead while introducing the new interaction patterns.

Collaboration: partnered with the UX Research team on discovery and validation · handed off final specs to engineering via Zeplin with annotated interaction states and component mappings to the Samsung Design System.

Post-launch qualitative feedback cited visual confirmation as the primary driver of increased confidence. Users reported navigating with less friction and greater certainty they had selected the right product.

Key Learning

Research revealed identity confusion, not search intent.

Reframing the problem as a moment of confirmation · rather than a navigation failure · was what changed the design direction.

Small craft decisions · visual hierarchy, instant verification cues · compound into measurable business outcomes when they land at the right moment. The real leverage wasn't restructuring the navigation. It was inserting a confirmation signal at the precise point where users were abandoning.

Verification-first design outperforms navigation-first design when the user's real problem is identity confirmation · not discovery.

What I'd Test Next

Honest limitation: validation happened post-launch against behavioral data · not through pre-launch usability testing with real users. Some design choices weren't fully de-risked before ship.

Next iteration would run moderated usability sessions earlier to validate the taxonomy before implementation · and layer in proactive model-detection (via serial number or purchase history) to collapse selection time toward zero for returning users.
